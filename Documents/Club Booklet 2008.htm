LEARNING TO SAIL  2008

 

As you are all aware it is the Club’s objective for 2008 to attract new members into the club. This is important to the life of the Club. It not only ensures the club stays afloat but also brings in new blood.

 

One of the ways of achieving this is to train people who would like to take up sailing. However over the last few years this has been limited - not by lack of people wanting to learn to sail, but lack of resources (people) to train them.

 

The club needs not only more qualified Instructors or Assistant Instructors but also a team of enthusiastic helpers. These helpers are needed to support the instructors by driving the safety boat, helping to rig the boats to get the trainees out on the water, and give simple advice to the trainees.  Each beginner course lasts for approximately 12 hours usually spread over 4-6 weeks, with the aim that the trainees achieve RYA level 2 at the end. Some basic race training is then provided for those wanting to continue to develop their experience.

 

With the help of funding from Corus we have improved the training fleet for 2008 - new sails and rigging for Toppers, general repairs and the acquisition of two Optimists.

 

The club premises are available any evening for training as long as a team is available to run a session. So if there are any volunteers who can give up a few hours a week of their time to pass on their love of sailing to others please contact Gordon Evans, Peter Martin, Jeremy Martin or Matt Sayer. Telephone numbers are on page 40.

HEALTH AND SAFETY MATTERS

 

Committee members have assessed the risks associated with our activities and have agreed on a series of actions, some now completed, some on-going.  These actions are designed to remove or minimise the risks to the safety and health of members, visitors and guests, and to the security of individual and club equipment.  Full details are posted in the clubhouse.  The following guidelines are of particular importance, and all members are asked to follow them :

 

·      Please cooperate with the plans to increase the level of rescue boat competence.

 

·      When on race duty, make sure that contact is     maintained between rescue boat crew and race     officer, normally by radio.

 

·      Do not leave your launching trolley in the water after launching your boat.

 

·      Ensure that your boat is left securely tied down in the compound to stakes which should not protrude above ground level.

 

·      Do not smoke in the clubhouse, or in or around the fuel store or rescue boats.

 

Members are invited to read the posted Risk Register, and to bring any comments or points of concern to any committee member.

Young persons

The Club has a young persons’ welfare policy posted in the clubhouse. Ruth Evans is the welfare officer for young people and can be approached on any such matters.

SAFETY AND RESCUE BOAT PROCEDURES

 

Sailing may not begin until safety cover is available according to the procedures below which describe the minimum provision.       Circumstances may require a higher level of provision to be agreed by the Race Officer and the most senior club officer or committee member present. Safety provision at a lower level will not normally be allowed and requires specific authorisation from the most senior club officer or committee member present after consideration of prevailing circumstances.

Boats providing safety cover for racing may not be used for any other purposes during racing.

 

Safety cover for training sessions is at a level decided by the Senior Instructor in charge at the time, who is then responsible for ensuring its provision.

The safety cover expected for casual sailing is given in the rules for casual sailing.

 

Boats and equipment

A minimum of one boat, launched and with engine warmed up, must be available before any racing. Two boats may be called for (see above), and this is required in the winter months (November to March inclusive) if the wind is Force 4 or above and there are more than 15 racing boats , or if the wind is Force 5 or above even for less than 15 boats.

Each safety boat must carry sufficient fuel for the session, a  canister with the basic safety equipment including First Aid kit, lines for towing, and a radio for communication with the Race Office. 

Each boat will be equipped with a kill-cord which must be used by the driver whenever the boat is underway. 

 

 

(10)

 

Personnel

Normally each safety boat should have a crew of two, a driver and an assistant. The driver must be on the club’s list of approved drivers (see next page), unless supervised by a powerboat instructor. Members under the age of 16 may drive with the same restrictions, but must be accompanied by a competent adult and with approval from a committee member.

All safety boat personnel must wear personal buoyancy and be dressed suitably for the conditions. In winter months, this means full wetsuits or drysuits.

 

Procedures

Procedures should be consistent with RYA guidance as given in Powerboat level 2 and Safetyboat courses. The following points are particularly important-

- safety boat personnel must monitor the race area constantly and should not engage in other activities;

- priority is to assist people; dinghies will be towed        provided that conditions permit;

- approach a dinghy in difficulties carefully, normally from downwind, taking account of likely effects of wind and  possible sudden righting;

- approach people in the water slowly and engage neutral before making contact; turn off the engine before           recovering the person if it is safe to do so.

It is not always necessary to attend every capsize, but every capsize should be observed carefully and attended if there is any cause for concern, bearing in mind the conditions and the age and experience of the sailors involved.

Safety boats must be in regular communication with the Race Officer who may give instructions. This means that a radio should normally be carried in each safety boat.

Guidance on operating the boats and engines is given in the Notes from the Bosun in this booklet.

Approval of drivers

The committee will arrange for a list of approved drivers to be maintained.  Approval will be given to club members who have the RYA PB2 certificate (or higher PB qualifications) or an equivalent experience, following a short session with a club Power boat instructor to ensure an understanding of club practices.  Training will be provided for members with lower levels of experience and this will lead to approval following satisfactory practical performance.  More advanced training can be arranged for those wishing to progress to the standard expected for providing cover in major events.

RACE OFFICER’S DUTIES

 

All members of the club over the age of 16 are expected to share in the race duties. This includes being race officer, assistant race officer or rescue boat driver/crew.

 

To ensure club racing is run efficiently, the following procedure will operate for 2008. A small sub-committee will allocate duties. The rota for the season is now contained in this booklet. Reminders will be sent out about 2 weeks before each duty. Members are expected to carry out the allocated duty, or make arrangements to exchange the duty with another person of suitable experience.

 

Members are invited to indicate dates when they will, or will not, be available for duties. A year planner is posted in the clubhouse every Nov-Dec, and members are encouraged to use this to provide information. All reasonable requests will be met.

 

Any member not attending a duty, or making suitable             arrangements to cover it, will be allocated 2 further duties in the next allocation period. These must be carried out. Any member who fails to attend the additional duties may be suspended from the Club by the Management Committee and will be unable to participate in Club activities. There will be no refund of fees and no racing results awarded. Appeal may be made to the Management Committee for reinstatement there where is good reason.

DINGHY COMPOUND

 

Your club fees entitle you to two free compound spaces.

 

      COMPOUND MANAGER                                              

      Sean Carter

      Seanc300@aol.com

      Tel:  01443 699262

 

 

COMPOUND RULES.

1.   All positions are allocated by the Compound Manager. When the club booklets are issued in early March, members will be asked to register their boats and will be allocated a 2008 sticker for each boat. Members should collect the sticker and display it before 31 March. After that date any boat without a sticker will be deemed abandoned and appropriate steps will be taken. (see para 7 below).

       Spaces not taken up by the 1st June may be reallocated. The aim is to make best use of the compound for everyone.

 

2.   A compound plan will be posted in the Clubhouse. All subsequent movements or changes of boat must be agreed with the Compound Manager.

 

3.   All boats must be kept in their allotted position. If you find another boat in your position, inform the Compound Manager, move that boat and secure it with tie-downs. Do not make matters worse by occupying a wrong position.

 

4.   Housekeeping, i.e. keeping the grass cut around your compound space etc, is the responsibility of the boat owner. This is required for safety reasons.

 

(32)

 

5.   No trailers or trolleys are to be kept in the compound other than in your allotted boat position.

 

6.    Each boat must be securely pegged down at all times, even without the mast stepped.

       Boats should be secured by two side stakes each at least
0.8 m long hammered completely below ground with only rope tails protruding; boats with masts stepped must also have bows secured.

 

       Members are reminded to make sure that boats left in the compound during the winter months, when the wind tends to be at its strongest, are securely tied down. You will be liable for damage caused to any other boat should your boat blow over.

 

The Club Constitution defines actions to be taken if unauthorised boats are left without proper payment. The full wording is available in the Clubhouse, but this summary gives the main points.

       If fees for membership or boat parking become 3 months in arrears, the club may move the boat without liability for any damage. On giving one month’s written notice the club may then sell the boat, or if this is impossible because of very poor condition, break it up.

       Lapsed members who leave boats in the compound are expected to pay a parking fee.

PROCEDURE FOR THE O.O.D./RACE TEAM

 

The nominated O.O.D/Race Team is responsible for

finding a replacement in the event of being unable to

undertake their duty. It is not sufficient to inform the Management Committee. Check the Club Hand-Book for your duties. Read the RYA rules and Club Sailing

Instructions beforehand. Arrive early and prepare the Race Hut and equipment. Ensure that the rescue boat is operating properly, has sufficient fuel and safety

equipment, and is on station and manned by responsible personnel equipped with radios. Racing must NOT

commence without rescue facilities.   (see pages 10-11).

Open the entry sheet and ensure that the date, race title and the O.O.D.’s name are recorded on each sheet.

 

30 MINUTES before the start select the course, aiming at a beat for the first leg. State the number of laps to be sailed. When satisfied with the course, sign the board to confirm the course to be sailed. You may now only change the course by raising flag L with two sound

signals.

 

4 MINUTES before the start raise the class flag and give one sound signal. Using the automatic sound system, this will initiate sound signals at 2 minute intervals until told to stop (instructions in the race hut).

 

2 MINUTES before the start raise the P flag (Blue Peter) and give one sound signal. Racing rules now apply.

(34)

 

PROCEDURE FOR THE O.O.D. (continued)

 

ZERO lower the class and P flag and give one sound

signal.  This is the basic starting procedure. (one start)

 A full description is given in the Club Sailing

Instructions for each series. The flags denote the exact time and must coincide with the clock. The sound signal merely draws attention to the flags.

 

RECALLS ensure no boats are over the line at the start. If any boat (s) is over the line give a second signal ( within 5 seconds. of the start), leave the class flag at half-mast. It is the helmsman’s responsibility to respond, the sound signal warns the fleet of an early starter.

 

The offender must return clear behind the line and restart. Failure to do so will result in disqualification and the race sheet being marked accordingly by the O.O.D. The class flag must remain at half-mast  until all the early starters have correctly restarted or a reasonable time after the     O.O.D. has disqualified the offenders.

 

GENERAL RECALLS  when there are either a number of early starters (perhaps too many to be identified) or some mistake in the starting procedure, a general recall is

indicated by two sound signals. The class flag is replaced by hoisting the first substitute flag. That class/group shall then re-start after the remaining classes have started and will await a new warning signal.

 

 

(35)

 

PROCEDURE FOR THE O.O.D. (continued)

 

The next phase consists of noting on the entry sheet each lap completed by each boat.

Particular attention should be paid to this point, with a large fleet it is better to use a Lap Result Sheet for this purpose.

 

As each boat completes its last lap to finish its race it must be given a  sound signal and that lap recorded on the entry sheet or Lap Result Sheet.

 

In Handicap racing it is necessary to record the finishing time of each boat in order to calculate its finishing

position.

 

GENERAL INFORMATION

Where there are two starts in handicap races, the fast fleet will have a PY less than 1086 and the slow fleet a PY greater than 1087.

 

Casual sailors must report to the O.O.D. before launching and keep clear of the racing area at all times, particularly the start and finish lines.

 

Officers of the Club are empowered to stop individuals from sailing if conditions are considered too severe.

NOTES FROM JEREMY MARTIN, THE BOSUN

Before Launching Checks

Check the Yamaha engines for oil level and top up if low.

The oil reservoir can be found under the engine cover. If in doubt, ask. The Mariner engines are 4 stroke, the oil level to check is the oil in the sump. The dip stick is on the side of the engine yellow in colour. Check the fuel level in the tank and top up if low.

The Yamaha and Mariner engines take straight petrol and the Johnson engines a 50:1 mixture.

Check the Dorys are drained of any water and that the bungs are in place. Check the kill cord is fitted, otherwise the engines will not start.

Once launched lower the engine into the vertical and lock down. The lock-downs are situated in the centre front of the engine under the choke.

To Start the Engines Yamaha and Johnson

Prime the engines by squeezing the bulb on the fuel line.

Check the control lever is in neutral. Select half throttle on the control lever.

If in doubt how to select the throttle only, seek advice.

If after a few pulls the engine doesn’t fire, then apply the choke. For the Johnson 30 this requires pumping.

Once the engine has started push in the choke and reduce throttle to a fast tick over.

Check the cooling water is circulating by looking at the rear left hand side of the engine, there should be a jet of water going down.

If there is no jet of water STOP the engine and seek advice.

 

(37)

 

The Mariner 4 stroke engines

These will not start unless the control lever is in neutral. The engines have auto chokes, just turn the ignition key and if needed the manual throttle put on. This is a small lever over the control box. Once the engine has warmed up return the throttle to its normal running position. The rescue boat should not be used until the engine is fully warmed.

Using the Engines.

To engage forward gear, ease the throttle lever slowly forward,  building up speed in a controlled manner.

Once the boat is on the plane, the throttle can be eased back about a quarter without loss of speed. This saves fuel. To engage reverse, the engine MUST be in neutral and the revs slowed down to idle, then with one smooth and positive move push the lever into reverse in one quick movement.

N.B. The engines are fitted with a dog clutch, so if you ease the lever into reverse the gear will jump and cause damage. It must be a firm movement. If the engine suddenly slows down while in use, check if a red light appears on the panel on the front of the engine. The reduction in revs is due to low oil. The boat can be safely driven back to the jetty and the oil reservoir immediately filled up with oil.

Please note. These boats are not toys, but expensive pieces of safety equipment. If it breaks down you  could be at risk.

Please report any faults to the Bosun or any Committee member straight away.
